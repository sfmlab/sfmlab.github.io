Research vision

Our research program combines coordination and analytical chemistry, focusing primarily on, but not limited to, nanoporous materials for liquid-phase sorption processes. This includes sensor chemistry, functional materials for analytical applications, and separation science. More specifically, we work with the design and application of metal-organic frameworks. Metal-organic frameworks (MOFs) are hybrid materials that form via the self-assembly of inorganic nodes with organic linker molecules. This yields highly ordered network solids that extend in up to three dimensions. Metal-organic frameworks are characterized by their high porosity, contact area, and modularity. By understanding structure-property relationships, we can tailor materials for specific applications, for example, sensing by analyte enrichment and background exclusion. We apply a variety of gravimetric and spectroscopic techniques to probe these interactions.

Furthermore, the group is interested in advanced multi-dimensional chromatography combined with high-resolution mass spectrometry to probe complex systems, e.g., environmental and process analytical technology. Here, a fundamental interest is the development of reactors and microfluidic devices that allow in-situ monitoring with a high level of automation.

Scientific dissemination

We believe that science paid for by public grants and foundations should be readily accessible to everyone immediately. At bare minimum, this means that all our manuscripts will be available as preprints (which rarely differ from the final peer-review edition, see REFERENCE). Furthermore, due to integrity issues, we avoid submitting manuscripts to the following publishers; Elsevier, MDPI and Frontiers (when corresponding authors).

Secondly, we believe that good science takes time and that we, as a community, should produce fewer papers of higher quality. Therefore, we spend time ensuring that our experiments are of the highest quality. This includes curation, indexing and sharing of all raw data generated at the time of publication. The data might not be perfectly organized, but it is there and freely available (not only on ‘reasonable request’).

Project Bifrost

We are involved in the work package on monitoring technologies. Here, our objective is to develop chemical sensors based on functionalized fiber optics. Seabed and shallow subsurface monitoring of offshore carbon storage sites. Multi-parameter sensor networks based on functionalized fiber Bragg gratings.
