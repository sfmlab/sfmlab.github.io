Roles and expectations

My role as supervisor

Research is to a large extent an adventure in trial and error and failed experiments. There is no manual available and I will not have the answer to every question you ask. My job as a supervisor is to provide the support required for you to carry out this task to the best extent possible. This means that I will provide scientific support, access to infrastructure and the means necessary to run and interpret experiments. I set high expectations and goals with work-life balance in mind. This means that I expect you to work hard and efficient within a 40-hour week.

My advice may be general, or specific. However, if I ask you to perform a specific task, it means that you should carry it out as soon as possible. If you disagree, convince me otherwise. I might very well be wrong (happens a lot). Ultimately, you were employed to carry out a specific research project. No matter if you are a Ph.D. student or postdoc, this is a job. You that have the main responsibility for the success of this project. I’m just the support staff.

Meetings

Standup

Every Monday at 09.00 we have a joint standup meeting. Here, we take turns to share planned activities for the coming week in an informal manner.

Supervisor meetings

We have scheduled group meetings on Thursdays at 13.00 every week. Each member is required to prepare a brief presentation of the current status, results and activities since last meeting. We will use this format to identify challenges, potential for collaborations and plan the experimental work efficiently.

Group meetings

We have joint meetings with the extended Experimentalists team which includes all researchers working in the lab facilities at DTU Offshore. These meetings are currently scheduled every second week on Thursdays at 10.00. Meetings include news and updates from our department head, scientific presentation and discussions of relevant happenings or issues. The aim of the meetings is to get academic and scientific support, identify potential overlaps between projects and individual members’ research interests. Ultimately, these meetings are a good opportunity to garnish collaborations and help each other to become better scientists.

My expectations from you

Be present and communicate

  • All group and subgroup meetings (including stand-up) are mandatory. Be on time.
  • Although working hours are flexible it is expected that you are present at DTU between 09 and 15 (unless having other obligations). This is to facilitate interaction and collaborations with other team members.
  • Deadlines are hard and I expect that you respect them. Delivering on time is important to maintain progress and ensure enough time for review, commenting and approval of drafts. If you cannot meet a deadline, communicate this as early as possible and we’ll adjust accordingly.
  • We are an experimental group. This means that the most of your time (>80%) should be spent in the laboratory setting up, running and characterizing experiments.
  • If I am not aware of an issue, I cannot help. Please seek me out as soon as possible, both for technical and other help.

Take ownership

  • I expect all members to take ownership of their project. You are the main driver for success, and your career future depends on it.
  • This means taking the responsibility to keep the project organized, making continuous progress, and finished as in running the necessary experiments, drafting manuscripts, reports and thesis (for Ph.D. students) in time.

Be prepared

  • Always come prepared. This is especially important for project and group meetings.
  • When discussing progress and challenges, you are expected to have prepared a presentation with detailed slides on results, data and figures.
  • Please remember that I juggle several tasks and projects. Therefore, I cannot remember everything. Although it is my obligation to also come prepared, I might ask questions that for you are clear but not for me.
  • Always consider your audience when preparing for meetings. A technical meeting with your supervisors is different than a project progress meeting with management.

Document everything

  • All laboratory notes must be documented in Labguru immediately. Using pen and paper is not allowed.
  • Each experiment must have a unique identifier. The unique identifier must follow all associated items (i.e. ELN, raw data files, result files).
  • Notebooks must be include documentation to the extent that it allows other researchers with similar training to repeat the experiment.
  • Small data files should (in addition to storage on the file server) be attached to the corresponding experiment. Larger files (> 100 mb) should only be stored on the file server (with a location reference included in the ELN entry)
  • Carry out regular backup of all written documents, data and similar. This is preferably done on your personal M-drive (NOT on the local drive of your laptop). Research data should be stored on the group network folder. (see the Research Data Management section)

Stay updated with the literature

  • Please keep updated with the relevant literature. Reading is an important part of science, and I recommend reading at least one paper daily.
  • This is important both to gain inspiration but also keep track of what already has been done. Do not reinvent the wheel.

My responsibilities to you

Be your advocate

  • I will support you in meetings, during presentations, in the lab and defenses.
  • This also means that I will do my best to support your career decisions, whether it be a future in academia or industry.

Be available

  • I am available on a daily basis and have an open door policy. You are always welcome to come by, or send an e-mail. Do not hesitate in contacting me no matter how big or small the matter.
  • On occasion where I am out of office, it is always marked in my Outlook calendar. Please add it to your computer to keep track of my whereabouts.

Provide research and project support

  • It is my job to support you in the development of your research project, both in terms of soft and hard skills.
  • This includes providing direction on where to get additional help when needed.

Assist with writing/editing papers, posters

  • As a principle, the first paper as a Ph.D. student will typically be drafted together.
  • For second publications and postdocs, the first draft is your responsibility. However, this does not mean that I will not provide support.
  • In order for me to do this thoroughly I should have drafts in good time before the submission deadline. This typically means at least two weeks in advance.
  • Remember that proofs with a large number of corrections and comments should not be taken as a critique, but as an indication that I take this job seriously and am here to help you develop.

Expand your network

  • A large part of the responsibility as a researcher is to build a network. This can be daunting and a difficult task. It is my responsibility to aid you in this task, both by providing access to my network but also to help you identify potential new collaborators.
  • Several parts of your project may require facilities and expertise not available in-house. It is my job to direct you where to get the help required to carry out all relevant work.

Provide funding and help with budgets

  • You should never worry about the costs of running your project. This is my responsibility.
  • If additional funds are required, e.g. for purchase of medium-sized equipment, I will make sure that this is made available.
  • There are several possibilities to apply for internal/external funding (both for Ph.D. students and postdocs) during your stay at DTU. I am here to help you find the relevant opportunities as well as writing grant applications.
